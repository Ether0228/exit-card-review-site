  1. A. Yes, because opposite vectors are always parallel.The conclusion does not follow; parallel vectors can also be equal.
  2. B. No, because parallel vectors can also be equal.Correct! Parallel vectors can have the same direction (equal) or opposite direction.

  4. D. No, because opposite vectors are not parallel.Opposite vectors are indeed parallel.
Q9 / KN2 / E1 / distinguish / understand L2

Explain the difference between a scalar and a vector. Give one example of each that was discussed in the lesson.

Rubric: Correct response: Scalar has only magnitude (e.g., distance, height, mass). Vector has magnitude and direction (e.g., displacement, velocity, force). Examples from lesson: height of a person (scalar), velocity 5 m/s east (vector).

Sample strong answer

A scalar is a quantity that has only magnitude, such as the height of a person (175 cm). A vector has both magnitude and direction, such as a car traveling at 60 km/h east. Scalars tell you 'how much,' while vectors tell you 'how much and which way.'

Q10 / KN4 / E2 / construct / apply L3

Refer to the figure showing vectors \(\overrightarrow{PR}\) and \(\overrightarrow{RQ}\). Are these vectors equal? Justify your answer by comparing their magnitudes and directions.

Rubric: Correct response: No, they are not equal. Magnitude of PR = sqrt(1^2+3^2)=sqrt(10) ≈ 3.16 units, direction is down and right. Magnitude of RQ = 3 units, direction is right. Different magnitudes and directions, so not equal.

Sample strong answer

No, the vectors are not equal. Vector PR has a magnitude of √(1²+3²)=√10≈3.16 units and points down and to the right. Vector RQ has a magnitude of 3 units and points directly to the right. Since both the magnitudes and directions differ, the vectors are not equal.

补救模块

E1

Student cannot distinguish between scalar and vector quantities.

Mini lesson: A scalar is a quantity that has only magnitude (size). A vector has both magnitude and direction. Think: scalar = how much; vector = how much and which way.

E2

Student incorrectly identifies equal vectors, often ignoring direction or magnitude.

Mini lesson: Two vectors are equal if they have BOTH the same magnitude (length) AND the same direction. If either differs, they are not equal.

E3

Student misidentifies opposite vectors, confusing them with equal or unrelated vectors.

Mini lesson: Two vectors are opposite if they have the SAME magnitude but OPPOSITE direction. They are parallel but point opposite ways.

E4

Student thinks parallel vectors must be opposite, or does not understand that parallel includes both same and opposite directions.

Mini lesson: Parallel vectors have the same or opposite direction. They lie on the same line or parallel lines. Opposite vectors are a subset of parallel vectors. Equal vectors are also parallel (same direction).

E5

Student misinterprets the tail-head order of a geometric vector.

Mini lesson: In vector AB, the first letter is the tail (starting point), the second letter is the head (ending point). The vector points from A to B.

E6

Student struggles to identify equal/opposite vectors on a diagram, possibly due to spatial reasoning or misreading labels.

Mini lesson: On a diagram, vectors along parallel lines with the same direction are equal if they have the same length. Use the labels to determine direction: vector from tail to head.

E7

Student misunderstands true bearing: measures from wrong reference or wrong direction.

Mini lesson: True bearing is the angle measured clockwise from true north, always written with three digits (e.g., 045°). North = 000°, East = 090°, South = 180°, West = 270°.
