Q1. A foreign company says China's high-speed rail and port network can reduce logistics time. In a SWOT analysis of investing in China, this is best classified as:

  1. A. A strength (internal to the company)
    This is an external condition of the country, not a capability of the investing company.
  2. B. A weakness (internal to the company)
    This is a positive factor, not a weakness.
  3. ✓ C. An opportunity (external favorable condition)
    Correct. The infrastructure is an external market condition that can benefit the investing company.
  4. D. A threat (external unfavorable condition)
    Infrastructure is helpful, not harmful, to investors.

Q2. Which pair correctly separates internal and external factors for a company deciding whether to enter China?

err-internal-external-boundary | analyze | L2

  1. ✓ A. Internal: company's brand reputation; External: China's consumer trends
    Correct: brand reputation is a company asset (internal), consumer trends are market conditions (external).
  2. B. Internal: China's labor costs; External: company's technology
    Labor costs are external, technology is internal – the categories are swapped.
  3. C. Internal: company's management team; External: company's financial situation
    Both management and financial situation are internal factors.
  4. D. Internal: China's trade policies; External: company's product quality
    Trade policies are external, product quality is internal – the categories are swapped.

    补救模块

    err-swot-category-confusion

    Students know the terms Strength, Weakness, Opportunity, Threat, but they often classify any positive factor as a Strength, regardless of whether it is internal or external. For example, they might label China's excellent infrastructure as a Strength of the investing company, when it is actually an external Opportunity.

    Explain: SWOT analysis divides factors into two dimensions: helpful vs. harmful and internal vs. external. Strengths and Weaknesses are internal—they are capabilities or shortcomings of the organization itself. Opportunities and Threats are external—they are conditions in the market or environment. To classify correctly, ask: Is this factor about the company itself (internal) or about the outside world (external)? Then ask: Is it positive or negative?

    Example: In our lesson, we used McDonald's as a case. McDonald's strong brand recognition is a Strength (internal and positive). But a growing demand for fast food in a new country is an Opportunity (external and positive). If you say 'McDonald's can use China's high-speed rail for logistics,' that rail network is not a Strength of McDonald's—it's an external condition, so it's an Opportunity. Similarly, a new tariff policy that increases costs is a Threat (external and negative).

    Anchor: The 'Inside-Outside' Check: Before you label a factor, point to yourself for internal (strengths/weaknesses) or to the window for external (opportunities/threats). Then thumbs up for positive, thumbs down for negative.

    err-internal-external-boundary

    Students think that any positive factor about a country (like large market, good infrastructure) is automatically a strength of the company investing there. They don't distinguish between external market conditions and internal company capabilities.

    Explain: In SWOT analysis, 'internal' means factors the company can control (e.g., its brand, technology, management). 'External' means factors in the country or market that the company cannot control (e.g., size of population, infrastructure, government policies). Strengths and weaknesses are internal; opportunities and threats are external. So, China's infrastructure is an external opportunity for the investing company, not a strength of that company.

    Example: McDonald's case: McDonald's owns its brand, recipes, and supply chain (internal strengths). But China's rising middle class and growing demand for fast food are external opportunities. McDonald's cannot 'own' the middle class; it can only take advantage of it.

    Anchor: Whose factor is it? If it's the country's, it's external – an opportunity or threat. If it's the company's, it's internal – a strength or weakness.

    err-unsupported-claim

    The student thinks that stating a confident opinion or listing general advantages is enough to convince an investor, and does not realize that business claims require specific, credible data to be persuasive.

    Explain: In international business, a claim becomes strong only when it is backed by specific, credible evidence. Vague words like 'big', 'cheap', 'good' lack precision and don't help an investor make a decision. To build a persuasive argument, you must: (1) state your claim, (2) support it with official data, statistics, or policy details, and (3) explain why that evidence matters to the investor. For example, instead of saying 'China has a large market', say 'China has 1.4 billion consumers, with retail sales growing 8% annually (National Bureau of Statistics), which means high demand potential for foreign brands.'

    Example: In our lesson, Zhuofan Yu said 'China's living costs are cheap' but didn't provide cost-of-living comparisons with Western countries. Catherine Wang asked him to add specific data, such as 'Cost of living in Shanghai is 40% lower than in New York, according to Numbeo 2025 data.' Without that comparison, the claim is weak and unconvincing to investors.

    Anchor: Remember the 'Claim + Data + Why' triangle: Every claim needs a Data point (number, source, comparison) and a Why that links it to investor benefit.

    err-investor-perspective-missing

    Student thinks a persuasive business presentation just lists advantages; they do not realize that foreign investors weigh risks and concerns before deciding.

    Explain: When making a business case to attract foreign investors, you must address both the opportunities and the risks or concerns they care about. Investors evaluate not only advantages but also policy reliability, exit flexibility, costs, labor conditions, and return potential. If you only list advantages, your argument looks one-sided and unconvincing. You need to proactively address investor concerns to build trust.

    Example: Felix presented China's state-directed economic model as an advantage. Catherine Wang noted that the presenter should also address investor concern about exit flexibility—whether an investor can easily withdraw their money if conditions change. A stronger presentation would acknowledge that concern and provide evidence of policy stability or exit options.

    Anchor: Think like an investor: 'What could go wrong?' Not just 'What is great?' Address the risks to make your case credible.

    err-vague-business-language

    Students think that general positive adjectives like 'big' and 'cheap' are enough to persuade investors, not realizing that investors need measurable data, comparisons, and specific policy details to make informed decisions.

    Explain: Vague business language uses words like 'good,' 'big,' 'cheap,' 'fancy,' or 'helpful' without specific definitions, data, or context. Investors need concrete evidence: market size in numbers, cost comparisons with other countries, official policy details, and measurable benefits. Replacing vague words with specific facts makes your argument credible and persuasive.

    Example: In Shi Shangyi's presentation, he described products as 'fancy goods' and claimed 'good infrastructure.' Catherine Wang advised him to specify product categories and provide policy details or official statistics, such as 'China's high-speed rail network covers 42,000 km' and 'the negative list for FDI has been reduced to 33 items.'

    Anchor: Vague = Virtual promise; Specific = Sold deal.
