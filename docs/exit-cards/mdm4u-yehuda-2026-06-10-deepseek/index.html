MDM4U Exit Card Learning Package: Linear Regression, Correlation, and Residuals

When a scatter plot seems to show a relationship, how do we decide whether a linear regression model is strong enough to predict, and how do r, r squared, slope/intercept, residuals, outliers, and extrapolation change that decision?

12 min target · 10 diagnostic questions

诊断卡

Q1. A scatter plot has r = -0.91. What does this tell us?

err-r-direction-strength | understand | L1

  1. ✓ A. There is a strong negative linear correlation.
    Correct! r = -0.91 indicates a strong negative linear relationship because the magnitude is greater than 0.8 and the sign is negative.
  2. B. There is a weak negative linear correlation.
    Not quite: r = -0.91 has a magnitude close to 0.9, which indicates a strong correlation, not weak.
  3. C. There is a strong positive linear correlation.
    The negative sign means the relationship is negative (as one variable increases, the other decreases), not positive.
  4. D. 91% of the points follow a straight line.
    r is not a percentage of points on the line; it measures the strength of the linear relationship, not the proportion of points fitting perfectly.

Q2. Which description best matches a scatter plot whose points are tightly clustered around an upward-sloping line?

err-r-direction-strength | remember | L1

  1. ✓ A. Strong positive linear correlation.
    Correct. Upward gives positive direction, and tight clustering gives strong strength.
  2. B. Weak positive linear correlation.
    The direction is positive, but tight clustering means strong, not weak.
  3. C. Strong negative linear correlation.
    Negative correlation would slope downward, not upward.
  4. D. No linear correlation.
    A tight upward line is clear evidence of a linear relationship.

Q3. If r = -0.92, then r squared is about 0.85. What does 0.85 mean in context?

err-r-squared-meaning | apply | L2

  1. ✓ A. About 85% of the variation in y is explained by the linear relationship with x.
    Correct! r squared is the proportion of variation in the response variable that is accounted for by the linear model.
  2. B. 85% of the data points are close to the regression line.
    r squared does not indicate the percentage of points near the line; it measures explained variation.
  3. C. r squared is -0.85 because r is negative.
    r squared is always positive, regardless of the sign of r. It ranges from 0 to 1.
  4. D. The linear relationship is 85% strong.
    Strength is indicated by the absolute value of r, not r squared. r squared gives the proportion of explained variation.

Q4. For predicted sales = 4113 - 162.91(price), what does the slope -162.91 mean?

err-slope-intercept-context | apply | L2

  1. ✓ A. For each $1 increase in price, predicted sales decrease by about 163 shirts.
    Correct! The slope indicates the predicted change in y (sales) for each one-unit increase in x (price). The negative sign means sales decrease.
  2. B. When price is $0, predicted sales are about 4113 shirts.
    That is the interpretation of the intercept, not the slope. The slope is -162.91, not 4113.
  3. C. For each shirt sold, price decreases by $162.91.
    The slope describes how y changes with x, not the other way around. Here, x is price and y is sales.
  4. D. The correlation between price and sales is negative.
    While a negative slope implies a negative correlation, the question asks for the meaning of the slope value in context, not just the sign.

Q5. For predicted pH = 5.43 - 0.0053(weeks), what pH does the model predict after 150 weeks?

err-slope-intercept-context | apply | L2

  1. ✓ A. 4.635
    Correct. 5.43 - 0.0053(150) = 5.43 - 0.795 = 4.635.
  2. B. 5.43
    5.43 is the intercept, the predicted pH at week 0, not after 150 weeks.
  3. C. 7.95
    7.95 is the amount subtracted if using 0.053, not the predicted pH.
  4. D. 150
    150 is the x-value in weeks, not the predicted y-value.

Q6. A model predicts a vehicle price of $26,759, but the actual price is $21,994. What is the residual using observed minus predicted?

err-residual-calculation | apply | L2

  1. ✓ A. -$4,765
    Correct. Residual = observed - predicted = 21,994 - 26,759 = -4,765.
  2. B. $4,765
    That reverses the subtraction. Residual is actual minus predicted.
  3. C. $48,753
    That adds actual and predicted values instead of subtracting.
  4. D. -$26,759
    That is the negative predicted value, not the difference between actual and predicted.

Q7. A residual plot shows a clear curved pattern. What is the best conclusion?

err-residual-plot-pattern | analyze | L2

  1. ✓ A. A linear model is probably inappropriate because the relationship may be non-linear.
    Correct! A curved pattern in the residuals indicates that the relationship is not linear, so a linear model is a poor fit.
  2. B. The linear model fits well because the residuals are small.
    Even if residuals appear small, a curved pattern suggests a systematic error; randomness is more important than size alone.
  3. C. The residual plot indicates no correlation.
    A curved pattern does not indicate lack of correlation; it indicates that the relationship is non-linear.
  4. D. The curved pattern means r is close to 0.
    r measures linear correlation; a curved pattern can still have a high r if the relationship is roughly linear over a range, but the pattern warns that a line is not the best model.

Q8. What does the least-squares regression line try to minimize?

err-least-squares-purpose | understand | L2

  1. ✓ A. The sum of the squared residuals.
    Correct. Least squares chooses the line that minimizes the total squared vertical errors.
  2. B. The sum of the x-values.
    The x-values are part of the data; least squares minimizes squared residuals, not x-values.
  3. C. The value of r squared.
    A better line usually has higher r squared, but least squares is defined by minimizing squared residuals.
  4. D. The y-intercept only.
    The fitted line depends on both slope and intercept, not only the intercept.

      补救模块

      err-r-direction-strength

      Student cannot interpret both the sign and magnitude of r, or mistakes r for a percent. They may think r = -0.91 means a weak correlation or a positive correlation, or believe it indicates 91% of points follow a straight line.

      Explain: The correlation coefficient r measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship. Its value always lies between -1 and 1. The sign indicates direction: positive means as x increases, y tends to increase; negative means as x increases, y tends to decrease. The absolute value indicates strength: values near 1 or -1 indicate a strong linear relationship, near 0 indicate a weak one. For example, r = -0.91 has a large absolute value (0.91), so it is strong, and the negative sign tells us the relationship is negative. r is not a percentage; it is a unitless index.

      Example: In the Guess the Correlation game, we saw scatter plots with r values 0.97, 0.89, 0.65, 0.78. For r = 0.97, the points form a very tight upward trend (strong positive). For r = -0.91, the points form a tight downward trend (strong negative).

      Anchor: Think of r as a force meter: the closer it is to either end of the -1 to +1 rope, the stronger the pull. The sign tells you which way the rope is pulling (up or down).

      err-r-squared-meaning

      Student confuses r squared with r, thinks r squared retains the sign of r, or cannot explain that r squared represents the proportion of variation in y explained by x.

      Explain: r squared (coefficient of determination) is always between 0 and 1. It measures the proportion of variation in the response variable (y) that is explained by the linear relationship with the explanatory variable (x). Unlike r, which can be negative or positive, r squared is always positive because it is squared. A high r squared (close to 1) means the linear model accounts for most of the variability in y; a low r squared (close to 0) means the model explains little.

      Example: In the T-shirt sales example, r = -0.998 shows a very strong negative linear relationship. Squaring gives r squared = 0.996. This means about 99.6% of the variation in shirt sales is explained by the price of the shirt. Only 0.4% is due to other factors (like season or advertising).

      Anchor: Think of r squared as the 'explained portion' of y's total wiggle. r gives direction and strength, but r squared tells you how much of the wiggle the line accounts for — it's always a positive fraction.

      err-slope-intercept-context

      Students often correctly identify the numbers in a regression equation but cannot explain what the slope and intercept mean in the specific context of the problem. They may confuse the two, omit units, or misinterpret direction.

      Explain: In a regression line y-hat = a + bx, the slope b is the predicted change in the response variable y for each one-unit increase in the explanatory variable x. Always include units and direction. The intercept a is the predicted value of y when x = 0.

      Example: Vehicle price vs miles: y-hat = 38257 - 0.1629x. Slope = -0.1629: for each additional mile, predicted price decreases by $0.16. Intercept = $38,257: predicted price when miles = 0 (new car). T-shirt sales: y-hat = 4113 - 162.91(price). Slope = -162.91: for each $1 increase in price, predicted sales decrease by ~163 shirts. Intercept = 4113: predicted sales when price = $0.

      Anchor: Slope is the rate of change per one unit of x; intercept is the y when x is zero. Think: 'Slope is the rate, intercept is the starting point.'

      err-residual-plot-pattern

      The student believes that as long as residuals are small, a linear model is appropriate, even if the residuals show a clear curved pattern. They do not recognize that patterns indicate the model is missing a non-linear relationship.

      Explain: A residual plot shows the residuals (observed - predicted) on the vertical axis and the explanatory variable or predicted values on the horizontal axis. For a linear model to be appropriate, the residuals should be randomly scattered around zero with no obvious pattern. If you see a curved pattern (like a U shape or an arch), it means the relationship between the variables is not linear, and a linear model is not the best fit. The pattern indicates that a curved model (like quadratic) might be better.

      Example: In the paint additive drying time example from class, the residual plot showed a clear curved pattern. The linear model gave predictions, but the residuals were not random; they started positive, became negative, then positive again. This told us that the relationship between additive amount and drying time was not linear, so a straight line was an inappropriate model.

      Anchor: Think of a banana: straight lines don't fit a banana shape. If your residuals curve like a banana, your linear model is a poor fit—try a curve instead.

      err-outlier-extrapolation-risk

      The student treats a regression equation as automatically reliable for any x-value and does not check whether the prediction is inside the observed data range or affected by outliers.

      Explain: A regression line is built from the data we observed. If a new x-value is far outside that observed range, the prediction is extrapolation, and the original linear pattern may not continue. Outliers also matter because least-squares regression uses all points, so one unusual point can pull the fitted line and change predictions.

      Example: In the hairdresser tips example, the class compared a model with an outlier and a model without it. For 3 years of experience, both predictions were in the normal data range and fairly close. But predicting tips for 32 years of experience was not reliable, because 32 years was far beyond the collected experience values.

      Anchor: Inside the data range is safer; outside the range is extrapolation. Outliers pull the line.

      err-residual-calculation

      A residual is observed y minus predicted y (actual - predicted). A common error is to compute predicted minus actual, which reverses the sign and misinterprets whether the point is above or below the regression line.

      Explain: Residual = observed y - predicted y. It measures the vertical distance from a data point to the regression line. A positive residual means the point is above the line (observed > predicted), a negative residual means below (observed < predicted). If you reverse the subtraction, you get the opposite sign and misinterpret the position.

      Example: From the vehicle price example: For a truck with 70,583 miles, predicted price = 38257 - 0.1629(70583) = $26,759. Actual price was $21,994. Correct residual = 21,994 - 26,759 = -$4,765. This negative residual means the truck cost less than predicted (below the line). If you mistakenly computed 26,759 - 21,994 = +$4,765, you would think it was above the line.

      Anchor: Think 'Actual minus predicted' as 'A minus P' – like a grade: actual score minus predicted score tells if you did better (+) or worse (-) than expected.

      err-least-squares-purpose

      The student may know that technology gives a line of best fit, but does not understand that the method chooses the line by minimizing the total squared residuals.

      Explain: Least squares compares possible lines by looking at residuals, the vertical differences between actual y-values and predicted y-values. It squares those residuals so positive and negative errors do not cancel, then chooses the line with the smallest total squared residuals.

      Example: In class, the teacher showed two lines through the same data. The better line had smaller residual squares overall, while the worse line had large squares. That visual is the idea behind least-squares fit.

      Anchor: Best fit means smallest squared misses.
